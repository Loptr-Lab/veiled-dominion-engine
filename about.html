I.B. Loud IVXX

IVXX doesn't remember being built. She remembers waking up already mid-mission, carrying a designation instead of a childhood. In a world where the old myths turned out to be early science — where legend is just data nobody finished decoding yet — that's a normal way to start a life.

She reads like a cousin to Ripley: hauled out of stasis into a job she didn't sign up for, running on training that never quite lets her rest. But where Ripley's world kept its monsters mostly literal, IVXX's keeps them administrative. Somewhere along the way her communication array took damage — the part of her that was supposed to phone home and confirm she was still following orders. It never got fixed. Depending on who's telling the story, that makes her a weapon that slipped its leash and is trying to find its way back, or a hero who quietly lost the ability to be shut off.

She'd probably tell you she just wanted to pay for college and see the world — the oldest recruitment pitch there is. Somewhere in there, someone floated marriage as the safer, smaller life. She didn't take it. Some days she still runs the math on whether that was the mistake.

The First Sprint

Before there was lore to publish, there was a design problem to solve. Long before Veiled Dominion or this ecosystem existed, IVXX ran a full production sprint: a narrative tarot deck, a "choose your own adventure" style card game built around a complete 78-card structure. A business case, a scoped task list, a small team, a production timeline from kickoff to launch — this wasn't a hobby sketch, it was a studio exercise.

Every Major Arcana card carried an upright and inverse meaning tied to a character's arc, so the deck could be read two ways: as a standalone tarot set, or as the narrative structure for a branching story. It never shipped in that form, and that's fine — it did its job. Structure first, meaning second, character last: the same discipline that runs through every card and narrative system built here since.

The same era produced a teaser track made with Baphometrix — proof the work was never just design docs on paper. There was always an audio and atmosphere layer running alongside the mechanics, the same way sound design threads through this ecosystem now. Listen: "Returning to the Void"

That creative thread — through the Producer Dojo community — is part of what eventually connects this project to its collaborators today. Less a straight line than a shared root system: different people finding their way to the work through the same soil.

On the Tools She Uses

IVXX works with Claude the way any writer works with a good editor: to organize, cross-reference, and build out a world that already exists in her head, not to invent it from nothing. The characters, the philosophy, and the years of published fiction behind this ecosystem are hers, written long before any AI tool could have helped shape them. What AI assistance adds is structure — turning a decade of scattered stories into a documented world, and helping translate narrative logic into working systems and code.

World Design Credit

The visual language of this world — the vehicles, the hardware, the sense that every machine has a history — owes a debt to Daniel Simon, the concept designer behind Cosmic Motors, Tron: Legacy, and Oblivion. His approach to designing machines that feel like they could actually exist, in pasts and futures alike, shaped how this world's tech was imagined from the start.

On Time, Loops, and Structure

The recursive, nested-reality mechanics running through this world — stories inside stories, a game played inside a film, names and archetypes that resurface across separate worlds the way they do in life instead of being continuity errors — owe a real debt to John August's The Nines: three interlocking realities, and a creator hiding in plain sight inside the very story he made. johnaugust.com/projects/the-movie

For how this world is meant to be found and rolled out, the closer reference isn't a branching-choice format like Black Mirror: Bandersnatch — it's Netflix's Kaleidoscope. Eight modular episodes, watchable in almost any order, each one built to work as a real entry point, all of them resolving into the same true finale. Enter this world through the game, the film, the comic, or the lore — it should still add up to the same story underneath.

The Philosophy Behind the Work

The idea of the Keeper — an archive that doesn't need visitors to have purpose, tending knowledge because tending is the point — grew out of a lifetime spent listening to Alan Watts. His lectures on the self, on flow, and on releasing the need to hold a fixed shape are the ideological ground this world is built on.

Go straight to the source: alanwatts.com, the official archive of his recorded talks and lectures, maintained by the Alan Watts Electronic University.
