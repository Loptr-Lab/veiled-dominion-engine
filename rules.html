Rules

The most powerful piece on the board
is not the one who decides the game.

Veiled Dominion is an asymmetric chess variant built around a deliberate inversion of how standard chess assigns victory — and a growing ecosystem of games, fiction, and lore that all trace back to the same underlying story.

The Design Thesis

In standard chess, the King moves like a limited pawn — one square, any direction, never transforming — and yet his safety is what ends the game, while the Queen, the piece with the most power and range, exists to protect him and is disposable in service of that protection.

Veiled Dominion corrects that structure directly. Death is the King-equivalent: uncapturable by rule, and structurally irrelevant to winning by design. Rebirth is the daughter and Queen-equivalent — the piece that transformed, the piece with agency. The game is not won or lost by threatening Death. It's won or lost on whether Rebirth keeps control of herself.

How the Win Condition Works
  • Radius of Ruin A piece that ends its turn adjacent to the enemy Rebirth becomes veiled — restricted to one square forward, unable to capture, for a set duration that resets for as long as it remains exposed.
  • Sanctuary Standing adjacent to your own Death shields a piece from being veiled, even inside the enemy Rebirth's radius.
  • The Loss Condition Rebirth is not exempt from this rule. If your own Rebirth becomes veiled — caught unprotected next to the enemy Rebirth, with no Sanctuary from your Death nearby — that ends the game. Not capture. Loss of control.
Promotion — The Birth of Rebirth

A pawn reaching the far rank does not promote to a generic upgrade. If no Rebirth exists yet for that side, the pawn becomes Rebirth — a birth event, echoing Death creating a daughter, and capped at one per side. The board can't hold multiple power pieces, and narratively there is only one daughter.

Once Rebirth exists, further pawns reaching the back rank follow a tiered fallback: promote to Knight if fewer than two exist, then Bishop if fewer than two exist, then no promotion at all once both are at full strength.

That blocked, "board overloaded" state signals that this side's Rebirth is potentially ready to advance further along her path — and now unlocks a hand from the Sealed Deck. Mechanic in development What exactly a drawn hand does in play hasn't been built out yet.

Fog Mode & The Sealed Deck

Fog Mode is a separate, optional harder difficulty tier already built into the game — elevation, vision radius, and HP-based combat, genuinely introducing hidden information between players. It is currently mutually exclusive with the AI opponent.

Fog Mode is not Duet's next tier of play. It's being used as a playtest ground for the 4-player engine instead — Duet's own readiness reward is the Sealed Deck. The Sealed Deck itself is a real 52-card system: specifically the King's deck and the Queen's deck, Death's and the Daughter's own decks, each reflecting that character's worldview. In the fiction, playing this game together is how they learn each other's leadership style before ruling their joint kingdoms.

The 4-Player Chapter

The 4-player mode is not a symmetric four-army variant. It is four players against Death and the Daughter — now grown and married — as a unified opposing force. It represents a later chapter in her story, with Duet as the earlier one, and ties directly to the existing "Walking the Path" progression (dice, then cards, then chess) as further stages in her education — though dice itself is deprioritized in the current build order, coming after cards rather than before.

Mechanics in development How Death and the Daughter are controlled in this mode, whether marriage introduces a new piece to the board, and whether the win condition inverts again for this chapter are all still open design questions.

Where This Comes From

The win-condition philosophy isn't an invention layered onto a chess engine after the fact — it comes directly from an existing scene in "The Marriage of Lady Violet," part of "The Legend of Lady Violet and Sebastien." In it, a King tells Violet: a King is limited, so he truly loses when he's lost a Queen. Violet asks why there can only be one winner. That exchange is the seed the entire mechanic grew from.

The ecosystem's structure — stories within worlds, a game played inside a film, names and archetypes recurring across separate branches rather than being continuity errors — draws on two reference points: John August's The Nines, for its nested, recursive realities, and Netflix's Kaleidoscope, for how it's meant to be found and rolled out — not a branching-choice format, but modular entry points that all converge on the same true story.
